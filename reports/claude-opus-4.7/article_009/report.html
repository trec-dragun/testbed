The US government is actually gearing up to share information about the “reality” of UFOs with the public — and not a moment too soon, says the man who claims to have run the Pentagon’s UFO program for 9 years.
Former President Donald Trump’s $2.3 trillion appropriation bill for 2021 contained a mandate that the Pentagon and spy agencies must file a report about “unidentified aerial phenomena” or UAP. Most of us just call them flying saucers or UFOs.
Whatever the jargon, noted whistleblower Luis Elizondo — former head of the Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program, which operated out of the secretive fifth floor of the Pentagon’s C Ring — told The Post about the resulting blockbuster document, which is reportedly slated for release in June.
Tied to the mandate, Elizondo said the upcoming report touches down on the unexplainable. Longtime UFO believers are hungry for explanations of the tic-tac-shaped objects the Navy encountered in 2004, the strange “cubes within spheres” seen by Navy pilots in 2014, or the mysterious black triangles continually reported around the world.
Such details promise to come via the much anticipated report — and at least one evolution of belief: “I think the government has acknowledged the reality of UAP,” Elizondo exclusively told The Post, despite signing what he refers to as a “lifelong” NDA before he resigned from the Pentagon in 2017. “I think they all want answers and I think they are all willing to ask the hard questions.”
‘This is not a conversation like fine wine where the longer we keep a cork on it, the better it gets.’
Luis Elizondo, former head of the Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program
During a press conference this week, Elizondo made clear that UFOs have been observed to have qualities that are nothing less than otherworldly. He described vessels flying at 11,000 miles-per-hour and being able to turn “instantly.” Providing a comparison, he explained, for our most advanced jets going at the same speed, “if you wanted to make a right-hand turn, it would take you about half the state of Ohio to do it.”
He also detailed Spielberg-worthy operational capabilities that fall into the realm of “transmedium travel.” Elizondo explained that the eye-popping vessels can fly 50-feet above the Earth’s surface or 80,000 feet in the sky and even submerge underwater without a compromise in performance: “When you see that, you recognize you are dealing with a technology more advanced than ours.”
That was despite apparent threats to security of the United States. Elizondo was particularly disturbed by the locations where UFOs or UAPs tended to hover.
Even the way in which these inexplicable flying-machines manage to lift-off blows away rational engineering. “ [These] things have no wings, no cockpits, no control surfaces, no rivets in the skin, no obvious signs of propulsion — and somehow they are able to defy the natural effects of Earth’s gravity,” Elizondo said. “How is that possible?”
The existence of the AATIP wasn’t revealed until 2017, along with what Trump described as a “hell of a video” montage captured by the Navy featuring a dark circular object flying in front of a military jet, along with another small object racing over land at astonishing speeds in 2004 and 2015, respectively. The Department of Defense confirmed the authenticity of the footage, and a Navy spokesman confirmed the objects in the videos to be UAP.
The Pentagon officially released three videos taken by US Navy pilots showing mid-air encounters with what appear to be UFOs. The grainy black and white footage had previously been leaked and the Navy confirmed in 2020 that they were authentic. The Department of Defense said it “was releasing the videos in order to clear up any misconceptions by the public on whether or not the footage that has been circulating was real, or whether or not there is more to the videos.”
DoD/AFP via Getty Images
At the time, former Senate Majority Leader Harry Reid (D-Nev.) took credit for arranging $22 million in annual funding for the AATIP, telling the New York Times that it was “one of the good things I did in my congressional service.”
By 2019, the Pentagon had confirmed to The Post for the first time that they research and investigate UFOs and continue to do so.

Meanwhile, UFO sightings in NYC were up 31% in 2020 — 46, compared to 35 the previous year — marking a whopping 283% spike from 2018’s dozen, according to the National UFO Reporting Center.
However, for generations, and for all the wrong reasons, national security bigwigs did not want to release more information to the public, Elizondo claims.
“They felt that it made them look inept,” he said. “They felt in some cases that it challenged their philosophical and theological belief systems … They just couldn’t process it.”
“There seems to be a very distinct congruency between UAP activity and our nuclear technology,” he continued. “That’s concerning to the point where we’ve actually had some of our nuclear capabilities disabled by these things … There is absolutely evidence that UAPs have an active interest in our nuclear technology.”
If this starts to sound like a “Twilight Zone” episode – Elizondo talks about UFOs pulling as many as 700 G-forces when our most technologically advanced aircraft can stand only 17 before falling apart (and that’s to say nothing about pilots at the controls) — it’s easy to hope for a fast and satisfactory wrap-up.
Clearly, that is what Elizondo is gunning for.
“This is not a conversation like fine wine where the longer we keep a cork on it, the better it gets,” he said. “This is a conversation like rotten fruit or vegetables in the refrigerator. And the longer it stays in there, the more it’s going to stink.”

The New York Post article, published April 24, 2021, frames a future Pentagon UAP report as an official acknowledgment that UFOs are 'real,' relying almost entirely on the on-the-record claims of Luis Elizondo, who is identified as a Pentagon whistleblower and former head of the Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program (AATIP).

nypost.com
2

The New York Post is a conservative-leaning tabloid daily owned by Rupert Murdoch's News Corp, so readers should weigh its single-source, dramatic framing accordingly.

en.wikipedia.org investors.newscorp.com
3

The article's central premise that 'Trump's $2.3 trillion appropriation bill' mandated a UAP report is broadly accurate: the Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2021 (Public Law 116-260) incorporated Senate Report 116-233 accompanying the Intelligence Authorization Act for FY 2021, which directed the DNI and SECDEF to deliver an unclassified UAP threat assessment.

congress.gov irp.fas.org www.dni.gov
4

The 'Preliminary Assessment: Unidentified Aerial Phenomena' was ultimately released by the Office of the Director of National Intelligence on June 25, 2021, roughly matching the Post's prediction of a June release.

www.dni.gov d3i6fh83elv35t.cloudfront.net
5

However, the actual ODNI assessment is far more cautious than Elizondo's framing in the Post, stating that 'the limited amount of high-quality reporting on unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP) hampers our ability to draw firm conclusions about the nature or intent of UAP' and that of 144 UAP reports only one was identified with high confidence — as a deflating balloon — with the rest remaining 'unexplained' rather than confirmed as exotic technology.

d3i6fh83elv35t.cloudfront.net en.wikisource.org
6

The ODNI report explicitly does not 'acknowledge the reality' of non-human or otherworldly UAP and instead lists five possible explanatory categories — airborne clutter, natural atmospheric phenomena, U.S. government or industry developmental programs, foreign adversary systems, and a catchall 'other' bin — contradicting the Post headline's implication of confirmed extraordinary phenomena.

d3i6fh83elv35t.cloudfront.net
7

Elizondo's foundational claim, repeated by the Post, that he 'ran' AATIP for nearly a decade is disputed by the Department of Defense, whose internal records and statements describe AATIP as having lost funding in 2012 and characterize Elizondo's later UAP work as informal coordination with 'no funding lines' and no formal program leadership.

www.theblackvault.com www.esd.whs.mil
8

Pentagon officials including the Director of Defense Intelligence concluded in an October 2017 memo that 'Mr. Elizondo's claims were not credible' after senior officials with knowledge of special programs could not substantiate his account of being secretly tasked by the Secretary of Defense to lead UFO investigations.

www.esd.whs.mil www.unredacted.info
9

The article repeats former Senator Harry Reid's claim that he arranged '$22 million in annual funding' for AATIP, but reporting based on FOIA records indicates that funding actually went to the related Advanced Aerospace Weapon System Applications Program (AAWSAP) contracted to Robert Bigelow's BAASS, a distinction Elizondo himself has emphasized.

www.theblackvault.com www.theblackvault.com
10

Specific performance claims attributed to Elizondo in the article — such as UAP flying 11,000 miles per hour, pulling 700 G-forces, executing transmedium travel, and disabling U.S. nuclear capabilities — are not corroborated by the ODNI preliminary assessment, which says it lacks sufficient data to confirm any breakthrough physics and that anomalous movement was noted in only 18 of the incidents reviewed and requires additional rigorous analysis.

d3i6fh83elv35t.cloudfront.net nypost.com
11

The Post's statistic that 'UFO sightings in NYC were up 31% in 2020' is sourced to the National UFO Reporting Center, a public crowd-sourced sightings database whose totals are not vetted government data and that includes only 46 reports for NYC in 2020 — a small base that makes percentage-change framing misleading without that context.

nypost.com
12

Missing context in the article includes that Elizondo, after leaving the Pentagon, joined Tom DeLonge's To The Stars Academy, which has a commercial interest in promoting UAP narratives, and that the DoD opened internal review of whether he may have mishandled sensitive information when classified-looking videos appeared publicly in 2017.

www.esd.whs.mil www.unredacted.info
13

Bottom line: the article accurately reports that Congress mandated a UAP report due around June 2021 and that the Navy confirmed authenticity of the released cockpit videos, but it overstates the significance of that report by uncritically channeling Elizondo's sensational characterizations, omitting the Pentagon's longstanding pushback on his AATIP-leadership claims, and predicting an 'acknowledgment of reality' that the actual ODNI assessment did not deliver.
